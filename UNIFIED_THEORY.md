# The Unified Theory of Grappling

> This document defines the conceptual framework that organizes all technique and position content on GrapplingWiki. Every article must be written with awareness of where its subject sits within this framework. This is what makes GrapplingWiki a knowledge system, not just a dictionary.

---

## The Gameplay Loop

Grappling is a game of **option compression**. Both players begin with equal and maximum options. Every successful action by the attacker removes options from the defender. The final state — zero options — is the tap.

### Phase 1: Neutral Standing

Both players are on their feet. Option space is equal and at its maximum. No grip contact has been made. This is the only moment of true symmetry in a match.

### Phase 2: Grip Contact → Grip Fight

First contact is made. The grip fight is a battle for **gripping asymmetry** — an advantaged grip scenario where one player controls more connection points, better angles, or higher-leverage grips than the opponent. The player who wins the grip fight gains the ability to predict and constrain the opponent's next actions.

**Grip fighting is the unifying micro-game.** It does not end when the match hits the ground. It is the constant thread running through every position, every transition, every exchange — standing and on the ground. The macro battle is positional; the micro battle is always grips.

### Grips vs. Frames: The Space Management Dichotomy

Every action in grappling can be understood through the lens of space management, and the two fundamental tools are grips and frames:

- **Grips remove space.** A grip is any connection that links you to the opponent and allows you to pull your center of mass closer to theirs — or pull theirs toward you. Gripping is inherently offensive. An underhook is functionally a grip: it links your body to theirs and helps you bring your hips and core closer.
- **Frames create space.** A frame is any structure (usually skeletal — forearms against the neck, knee against the hip) that pushes distance between you and the opponent when the position is not on your terms. Framing is inherently defensive.

The positional battle at every level is a grip vs. frame exchange. The attacker fights to establish grips that close distance; the defender fights to establish frames that create it. When the attacker's grips overcome the defender's frames, the distance spectrum collapses toward chest-to-chest and isolation becomes possible.

### Phase 3: Balance Disruption / Posture Compromise

The grip advantage creates opportunities to disrupt the opponent's balance (kuzushi) or compromise their posture, exposing angles of attack onto the four limbs or the neck. The opponent's option space begins to narrow.

### Phase 4: Takedown / Ground Transition

At some point, the exchange moves to the ground, creating a **top/bottom dynamic**. This may be initiated by the attacker (takedown, throw) or by the defender pulling guard. Either way, the symmetry of the standing exchange is replaced by an asymmetric positional hierarchy.

### Phase 5: The Top/Bottom Exchange — The Distance Spectrum

Once on the ground, all positions can be mapped along a **distance spectrum** measuring the space between the two players' chests:

- **Far end:** One standing, one on the ground. Maximum distance. Limited limb contact. Most options for both players.
- **Close end:** Chest-to-chest. Mount, smash half guard, side control. Maximum limb contact. Maximum control through positionally advantaged grips (underhooks, overhooks, head control).

As the top player progresses closer on the distance spectrum, they gain the ability to **wedge** — positioning their body between the opponent's limbs, breaking the opponent's structural cohesion.

### Phase 6: Isolation

The strategic objective of positional advancement is **isolation** — separating individual limbs or the neck from the opponent's body structure so they cannot generate connected, full-body force. An opponent generates maximum power when their hands and feet can connect and move as a unified kinetic chain. Breaking that chain is the precondition for finishing.

### Phase 7: Submission — Force Vector Application

Once a limb is isolated, the attacker achieves a **submission-ready configuration**: their entire posterior chain (or full body) engaged against a single limb, a combination of two limbs, or the neck plus a limb. The submission is completed by applying the correct force vector:

- **Extension/Flexion** — driving a joint past its comfortable range of motion (armbars, kneebars, Americana, Kimura)
- **Compression/Wedging** — inserting a fulcrum between bone segments to create tensile force that separates the joint structure (bicep slicers, calf slicers)
- **Torsion** — rotational force on the joint, especially prevalent in leg locks (heel hooks, toe holds)
- **Arterial Compression** — bilateral compression of the carotid arteries through various high-leverage configurations (chokes: RNC, guillotine, triangle, etc.)

### Submissions Are Endpoints, Not Transitions

Submissions sit at the terminal end of the causal chain. They are **endpoints, not transitory nodes.** You do not "flow through" a submission into the next technique — you either finish it or you fail it. A failed submission means the defender escaped or the attacker lost the position, and the exchange resets to a positional state — usually with the attacker having lost tempo.

This has critical implications for how the wiki structures its content and its technique graph:

- In the article graph and internal linking, submissions are **leaf nodes** — they link back to the positions they're available from, but they do not link forward to other techniques as if they're a waypoint.
- A "transition" out of a failed submission is not a flow — it is a consequence of failure, and the article should frame it as such (e.g., "if the armbar is lost, the attacker typically returns to closed guard or scrambles to re-establish position").
- The technique graph visualization should visually distinguish submissions from positions and transitions. Submissions are where causal chains terminate.

## The Physics of Top Position

Top position carries an inherent advantage from physics: stored **potential energy** that converts to kinetic energy against escape attempts. Gravity is a constant force multiplier. When the bottom player attempts to bridge, shrimp, or scramble, the top player can reposition their weight above and drop it through the optimal contact point to shut down the movement.

**Efficient control = real-time balancing.** The top player must continuously position their center of mass to create a force vector through their contact point that directly opposes whatever escape vector the bottom player generates. This is not static pinning — it is an active, dynamic balance game.

This physics creates a **heavy top bias** in the incentive structure: fight for top position → win grips → win leveraged underhooks/overhooks → progress on the distance spectrum → isolate → submit.

## The Orientation Axis: Face-to-Face vs. Face-to-Back

Beyond distance, ground positions have a second critical dimension: **orientation**.

- **Face-to-face:** Both players can see each other. Defensive tools (frames, guards, grip strips) are available. The defender has options.
- **Face-to-back (back exposure):** The defender cannot see the attacker. Defensive tools are severely limited. This is the worst-case positional scenario for the defender.

Back exposure is almost never a starting position — it is a **consequence of failed defense**. When a face-to-face attack becomes too threatening, the defender is forced to rotate away as a last-ditch escape, exposing their back. This makes back takes one of the highest-value positional achievements in grappling.

## Tempo and Option Space

Every exchange in grappling is governed by **tempo** — the rhythm of attack and defense.

- A successful attack forces the defender to spend their movement on defense.
- If the defense fails, the attacker advances further down the causal chain toward submission.
- If the defense succeeds, the defender has spent their turn — the attacker gets another opportunity to attack.
- A successful defense can also function as a counter-attack, flipping tempo entirely. The defender's escape may itself expose an attack on the original attacker.

**Option space narrows as the causal chain progresses.** From standing neutral (maximum options, equal for both) through grip fighting, positional advancement, and isolation, each successful action by the attacker compresses the defender's available responses. When you have fewer options, your next move becomes more predictable, which further advantages the attacker. The ultimate compression: zero options. Tap.

## Three Organizing Axes for Ground Positions

Every ground position can be mapped using three axes:

1. **Distance** — where on the chest-to-chest spectrum (far: one standing/one grounded → close: mount/smash half)
2. **Orientation** — face-to-face vs. face-to-back
3. **Grip State** — who controls the dominant grips (underhooks, overhooks, head control, collar ties, sleeve grips, etc.)

---

## How This Framework Applies to Articles

Every technique and position article on GrapplingWiki should answer:

1. **Where does this sit in the gameplay loop?** (Which phase? Which point on the causal chain?)
2. **Where does this sit on the three axes?** (Distance, orientation, grip state)
3. **What options does this compress for the opponent?** (What can they no longer do?)
4. **What options remain for the defender?** (What are the escape paths?)
5. **What is the tempo implication?** (Does this give the attacker another turn? Does it flip tempo?)
6. **What is the force vector?** (For submissions: extension, compression, torsion, or arterial compression?)
7. **What is the grip fighting micro-game within this position?** (What grips are being contested? What does winning/losing the grip battle mean here?)

This framework is what makes GrapplingWiki a **system of understanding**, not just a collection of definitions.

## Visual Architecture: Layer-Based Sorting

The gameplay loop defines distinct **layers of abstraction** in the grappling game. Articles, the technique graph, and all visual navigation should sort techniques into these layers — not alphabetically or by arbitrary category, but by where they sit in the causal chain.

### Layers

1. **Neutral / Standing** — takedowns, throws, guard pulls, clinch work
2. **Grip Fighting** — grip types, grip breaks, pummeling, hand fighting
3. **Disruption** — kuzushi, off-balancing, posture breaks, angle creation
4. **Positional Control** — all ground positions mapped on the distance spectrum and orientation axis
5. **Transitions** — sweeps, passes, escapes, scrambles — movements between positions
6. **Isolation** — the mechanisms that separate limbs from body cohesion
7. **Submission** — terminal endpoints, organized by force vector type

Each layer should have its own **color in the technique graph and navigation UI** so that a user can immediately see which layer of the game they're looking at. The color scheme should be defined in the Design Manifesto / style guide and applied consistently across the graph visualization, article tags, category pages, and any visual taxonomy.

**Graph structure:** Positions and transitions are nodes with bidirectional edges (you can move between them). Submissions are terminal leaf nodes with inbound edges only (you enter a submission attempt from a position; you don't flow out of one into another technique).
