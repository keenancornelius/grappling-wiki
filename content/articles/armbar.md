## Overview

The **armbar** — known in judo as **juji gatame** ("cross armlock") — is a hyperextension attack on the elbow joint. The attacker isolates one of the opponent's arms, controls the wrist, and uses the hips as a fulcrum to force the elbow past its normal range of motion. When applied correctly, the armbar threatens serious ligament damage in under a second, which is why it finishes at a very high rate across every ruleset in grappling.

Along with the [triangle choke](/wiki/triangle-choke) and [omoplata](/wiki/omoplata), the armbar is part of what Brazilian jiu-jitsu practitioners call "the three brothers" — a family of attacks from the guard that share geometry and constantly chain into each other. If you miss an armbar, the triangle is often there. If you miss the triangle, the omoplata is often there. Learning any one of the three deeply means learning all three.

The armbar is available from more positions than any other submission in grappling. It attacks from [closed guard](/wiki/closed-guard), from [mount](/wiki/mount), from [side control](/wiki/side-control), from [back control](/wiki/back-control), from [knee on belly](/wiki/knee-on-belly), from [north-south](/wiki/north-south-position), from the [turtle position](/wiki/turtle-position), from scrambles, and from standing. No other submission offers this breadth of entries. This versatility is why Ronda Rousey built an Olympic and UFC career on a single armbar, why Kyra Gracie finished world titles with it, and why every white belt in every BJJ gym learns it in their first month.

## How the Armbar Finishes

The armbar is not a strength move. A correctly positioned armbar finishes on a 250-pound opponent as reliably as it finishes on a 130-pound opponent, because the leverage is geometric, not muscular. The elbow is a hinge joint — it rotates on a single axis. When you bend a hinge past its range, the joint fails regardless of how strong the surrounding muscles are.

### The Four Requirements

Every finishing armbar, no matter where it comes from, needs four things to be true simultaneously:

1. **The opponent's arm is straight with the thumb pointing up.** If the thumb rotates down or the arm is bent, the hyperextension vector shifts and the elbow can escape. Wrist control that keeps the thumb pointed toward the ceiling is the single most important finishing detail.
2. **The hips are tight against the shoulder.** The closer your hip is to the opponent's shoulder joint, the shorter the lever arm they have to fight. Daylight between your hip and their shoulder is the number-one cause of failed armbars.
3. **The knees are pinched together.** Open knees let the opponent stack you, posture up, and start spinning out. Pinched knees keep the arm trapped and prevent the opponent from walking around your head.
4. **The hips drive upward as a unit.** The finish is a coordinated bridge — hip extension, not arm pulling. Beginners try to yank the arm; experienced grapplers barely pull at all and instead thrust the hips skyward.

When these four elements align, the finish happens with almost no pulling force. The opponent taps because their elbow is about to break, not because you overpowered them.

## The Armbar from Closed Guard

The closed guard armbar is the first armbar every grappler learns. It is also still one of the most effective in live training, even at advanced levels.

The classic entry starts with a cross collar or wrist grip to break the opponent's posture. Once the opponent's head is low, the attacker slides their hips out to a 45-degree angle — not staying flat on their back — and pulls the opponent's arm across their centerline. The leg on the far side of the trapped arm swings up and over the opponent's head, creating the top "leg of God" that traps the shoulder. The other leg clamps across the ribcage. Hips drive up, knees squeeze, wrist control stays clean — finish.

The critical detail most beginners miss is the **angle creation**. If your hips stay square to the opponent, the armbar is leveraging against their entire spine, which is far stronger than any lever you can apply. By turning your hips 90 degrees relative to theirs, you are leveraging against the small rotation of their shoulder, which cannot resist you.

## The Armbar from Mount

The mounted armbar is statistically the most dangerous version. From [mount](/wiki/mount), the attacker has the ability to post weight, isolate an arm without needing to break posture first, and transition to armbar by spinning on the hips while falling back. There is less time for the opponent to defend because there is no guard to pass, no posture to break — the attack is already in range.

Common entries from mount include:

- **The arm-trap swing.** When the opponent pushes up on the attacker's chest to escape, the attacker traps the extended arm with one hand, passes it across their own centerline, and swings the far leg over the head.
- **The Americana fake.** Setting up an [americana](/wiki/americana) forces the opponent to fight for wrist extension. When they do, the attacker lets the americana go and converts directly into an armbar on the same arm.
- **The S-mount transition.** From mount, sliding one knee up into the opponent's armpit creates an S-shaped position that loads the arm before the armbar. This is the Ronda Rousey sequence that defined her judo-to-MMA transition.

The finish from mount is slightly different from the guard version: instead of swinging the leg over from underneath, the attacker sits through, keeps their chest up, and finishes with the hips driving back rather than forward.

## The Armbar from the Back

A back-take armbar sequence is one of the highest-percentage finish combinations in modern jiu-jitsu. From [back control](/wiki/back-control), the attacker establishes a seatbelt grip and threatens the [rear naked choke](/wiki/rear-naked-choke). When the opponent defends the choke by bringing their arms up to fight for the neck, one of those arms is extended and vulnerable. The attacker traps it across the body, rolls to the far side, and finishes the armbar with the opponent still trapped on their back.

This sequence is a two-way threat: if the opponent defends the choke, the armbar is there; if they defend the armbar, the choke is there. The shared setup is what makes it so hard to escape.

## The Armbar from Standing

The **flying armbar** — hitting the armbar from a standing clinch by jumping and swinging the legs over — is the most spectacular version. It is rarely hit in competition, but when it is, it is one of the most visually striking finishes in all of martial arts. Fabricio Werdum's flying armbar on Alistair Overeem in Strikeforce and the many Shinya Aoki flying armbars in Japanese MMA are the canonical highlights.

More practically, **rolling junji gatame** from a standing clinch — a judo-derived entry — is a high-percentage finish that chains from kumi-kata grip fighting into a drop armbar. Imanari Kanehara's roll-under armbar is the no-gi cousin of the same idea.

## Common Mistakes

Most armbar failures come from the same handful of errors:

**Opening the knees.** The instant the knees separate, the opponent can stack, posture, walk around your head, and start the escape. Keep the knees pinched as if holding a basketball between them throughout the finish.

**Pulling the arm straight back.** The finish is the hips, not the pull. Pulling with your arms while keeping your hips passive gives the opponent time to bend their arm and defend.

**Losing wrist control.** If the thumb rotates, you lose the clean hyperextension vector. Always keep the thumb pointed up — two hands on the wrist if necessary.

**Letting the shoulder escape the hip.** Daylight between the hip and the trapped shoulder is fatal. Scoot your hips toward the opponent's shoulder every time you feel space appearing.

**Allowing the hand clasp (hitchhiker escape).** If the opponent interlaces their hands or grabs their own collar before you finish, they can often bend the arm and escape. Break any hand grips before committing to the finish.

**Going belly-down when the opponent stacks.** Going belly-down is a legitimate finish (the belly-down armbar), but it requires different mechanics than the on-the-back version. Mixing them up leads to weak finishes in both directions.

## Defending the Armbar

There is a clear defensive hierarchy for escaping an armbar, from earliest to latest:

1. **Don't let the arm get isolated in the first place.** Keep elbows glued to ribs when passing, mounting, or being mounted. The armbar needs an arm that has drifted away from the body.
2. **Bend the arm early.** Once you feel the attacker setting up the armbar, bend your elbow and grab your own collar or opposite biceps. A bent arm cannot be hyperextended.
3. **Stack and posture.** Drive your weight forward over the attacker, walk your legs toward their head, and try to climb your trapped shoulder over their hip. This is the classic "stack pass" style defense and it is the most common escape in gi BJJ.
4. **The hitchhiker escape.** If the arm is already straight and the hips are tight, turning the thumb toward the attacker's legs (like a hitchhiker) rotates the shoulder in a way that briefly lines up the elbow for an escape roll.
5. **Tap before the pop.** Armbar injuries are cumulative and career-shortening. Hyperextending once can turn into chronic instability. Tap early if the finish is locked and the defenses have failed.

## The Armbar in MMA

The armbar's MMA success is dominated by one name: **Ronda Rousey**. Rousey finished eight of her first thirteen professional fights with armbars — most in under a minute — by converting her Olympic judo throws directly into juji gatame before opponents could defend. Her version used a specific sequence: throw the opponent, trap the arm on the landing, isolate in S-mount, spin the leg over and finish before the opponent could even establish guard. It was the clearest demonstration of the armbar's speed and lethality in modern combat sports.

Other notable MMA armbars include Frank Mir's armbar of Tim Sylvia (which broke Sylvia's arm on live pay-per-view because Sylvia refused to tap), Miesha Tate's third-round armbar comeback, and Vitor Belfort's flying armbar of Jon Jones — a sequence Jones described afterward as the closest he had ever come to being finished.

## The Belly-Down Armbar

A variation on the standard juji gatame is the **belly-down armbar**, also called the **twister-side armbar** when entered from the back, where the attacker flips from their back to their stomach while retaining arm control. The hyperextension vector is opposite — the finish happens as the attacker bridges their hips upward with the opponent's arm over their shoulder. Belly-down is particularly effective when the opponent has a strong base, because stacking defenses that work against a face-up armbar do not work against a face-down one. The modern leglock specialists — Gordon Ryan, Craig Jones, Lachlan Giles — use belly-down mechanics frequently from leg entanglements as well.

## Training the Armbar

Drilling the armbar develops three distinct skills:

1. **Arm isolation.** Learning to get one arm away from the opponent's body, across their centerline, and pinned between your legs. This is the hardest and most under-drilled part of the armbar.
2. **Hip positioning.** Angle creation relative to the opponent's shoulder. Square hips equal failed armbars.
3. **The finishing chain.** Most armbars don't finish on the first attempt — they finish after a defense has been baited into another armbar or a [triangle](/wiki/triangle-choke) or [omoplata](/wiki/omoplata). Train the finish as a sequence, not a single move.

A good armbar drill is the "three brothers flow": from closed guard, attempt an armbar, have your partner defend by bringing their arm across, convert to a triangle, have them defend by turning, convert to an omoplata, have them defend by rolling out, and come back to guard to start again. Running this flow for three five-minute rounds builds the muscle memory that makes the armbar finish happen without conscious thought.
