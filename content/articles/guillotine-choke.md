## Overview

The **guillotine choke** is the most common scramble finish in all of grappling. It is a front-headlock choke where the attacker traps the opponent's head under one arm and applies pressure across the throat or the carotid arteries using the blade of the forearm. Unlike most submissions, the guillotine is available from positions that look terrible on paper — the bottom of a sprawl, the bottom of closed guard, the back of a snap down — and it finishes faster than almost any other choke in the sport when locked in.

In the positional hierarchy of grappling, the guillotine lives at the **[front headlock](/wiki/front-headlock-position)** combat zone — the face-to-back offensive mirror of closed guard. Whenever an opponent ducks their head, shoots a poor takedown, turtles, or gets snapped down, the guillotine is a step away. It is the reason wrestlers are taught never to drop their head when shooting in MMA: one bad level change against a guillotine specialist ends the fight.

The guillotine also shares DNA with a family of other front-headlock submissions — the [D'Arce choke](/wiki/darce-choke), the [anaconda choke](/wiki/anaconda-choke), and the peruvian necktie — all of which attack the same basic front-headlock position with slightly different arm configurations. Learning the guillotine means learning to read opponents who give up their posture, and the same reading applies to every front-headlock attack.

## How the Guillotine Choke Finishes

The guillotine is primarily a **blood choke**, not an air choke, when performed correctly. The blade of the attacker's forearm presses against one carotid artery while the attacker's own biceps or chest compresses the other. A true blood-choke guillotine finishes in the same 8-to-10-second window as a rear naked choke, with the opponent going unconscious rather than gagging on their own trachea.

Many beginners accidentally set up air chokes — pressing the bony edge of the forearm into the trachea, which is painful but slow. Air-choke guillotines can still finish against weak defenders, but they fail routinely against trained grapplers because the opponent can simply endure the discomfort and wait for an escape.

### The Four Requirements

A finishing guillotine needs four things to align:

1. **The forearm is under the chin, not on the throat.** The finishing line runs against the soft tissue at the top of the neck, angling up into the jaw. A forearm pressing straight into the Adam's apple is an air choke at best.
2. **The choking hand is buried behind the opposite shoulder.** The choke hand must disappear — either into the crook of the other elbow (standard guillotine grip), clasped in a Gable grip, or wrapped around the free biceps for the Marcelotine.
3. **The chest is pulled into the back of the opponent's head.** The finish is not just arm squeeze — it is the attacker pulling the opponent's head into their chest, effectively sandwiching the neck between the forearm in front and the chest behind.
4. **The opponent's posture is collapsed.** A guillotine on an opponent who is posturing up (head high, back straight) almost always fails. The head must be low and the spine curved forward.

## The Three Main Guillotine Variants

Modern grappling recognizes three main guillotine variants, each with its own optimal setup and finishing mechanics.

### The Standard (Arm-Out) Guillotine

This is the guillotine everyone learns first. The attacker's arm wraps around the neck with the opponent's arm **outside** the choke. The choke hand grips the opposite biceps or the wrist of the choking arm. Finish by pulling the opponent into the chest and extending the hips or body back.

The arm-out guillotine is the classic closed guard guillotine and the standard sprawl counter. It finishes reliably when the opponent's head is deep and low, but fails against opponents who keep their posture tall or hide their neck behind their shoulder.

### The Arm-In Guillotine

In the arm-in guillotine, one of the opponent's arms is **trapped inside** the choke, between the attacker's choking arm and the opponent's own neck. This configuration is common when the opponent shoots a single-leg and fails to clear the head — their shot arm stays on the attacker's leg, and the guillotine snakes in over the top of it.

The arm-in version is usually finished by rolling to the side rather than finishing straight-on, because the trapped arm acts as a wedge that prevents direct compression. The finish happens when the attacker gets to the ten-and-two position (their body at a 45-degree angle to the opponent) and extends the hips.

### The High-Elbow Guillotine (Marcelotine)

The **Marcelotine** — named for Marcelo Garcia, who refined and popularized it — is a high-elbow guillotine where the choking elbow is held up near the attacker's own ear, and the choking hand wraps the opponent's free biceps rather than the opposite shoulder. This configuration creates a tighter, more concentrated pressure on the carotid and is far harder to defend by posturing up.

The Marcelotine is devastatingly effective from butterfly guard, from half guard, and from the bottom of scrambles, because the high-elbow position works even when the opponent's posture is not fully broken. Marcelo Garcia finished hundreds of high-level no-gi matches with this choke, including in ADCC, where he remains one of the most decorated competitors in the event's history.

## The Guillotine from Standing

Standing guillotines are the most common MMA finishes from the clinch. When an opponent drops their head to defend a strike, shoots a bad takedown, or gets snapped down, the guillotine is a step away. The standing finish requires the attacker to lock in the grip quickly — usually a standard arm-out or Marcelotine grip — and then pull the opponent's head into their chest while stepping back to break the posture fully.

The critical detail in standing guillotines is **hip control**. If the opponent can grab the attacker's hips and drive forward, they can walk the attacker back into a cage or wall and escape the finish. Good standing guillotine finishers pull guard quickly, transitioning to closed guard or half guard before the opponent can use forward drive to escape.

## The Guillotine from Closed Guard

Closed guard is the classic terrestrial home of the guillotine. When an opponent postures forward with poor head position, the bottom player can shoot the guillotine arm across the neck and lock it in before the opponent has a chance to re-posture. The closed guard finish is easier than the standing finish because gravity is on the attacker's side — the opponent's weight naturally compresses their own neck into the choke.

The typical closed guard sequence is: break the opponent's posture with a collar grip, feel the opponent push forward with their head, snake the choking arm under the chin, grip the opposite biceps or clasp the hands, then either extend the hips or break the guard open and stretch the opponent out for the finish.

## The Guillotine as a Sprawl Counter

In wrestling-derived grappling, one of the most common guillotine setups is the **sprawl counter**. When an opponent shoots a takedown — particularly a single or double leg — and the attacker sprawls their hips back, the opponent's head ends up in the front headlock position, directly lined up for the guillotine. Wrestlers and BJJ players who have strong sprawls use this as their primary attack: they bait takedown attempts and finish guillotines on the counter.

The sprawl guillotine is almost always an arm-in variant because the opponent's shooting arm stays on the leg. The finish usually comes from rolling to the side (the "ten-and-two finish") or by converting into a [D'Arce choke](/wiki/darce-choke) or [anaconda choke](/wiki/anaconda-choke) if the guillotine grip doesn't materialize.

## Common Mistakes

Most failed guillotines share the same small set of errors:

**Choking the throat instead of the carotids.** A forearm pressed flat across the Adam's apple is painful but slow. The forearm must angle up into the soft tissue under the chin to compress the carotids.

**Elbow pointed to the side.** A flared choking elbow means the choke hand is too far from the centerline. The elbow should point straight down or slightly toward the opponent's feet for maximum compression.

**Grip too shallow.** The choking arm must be deep enough that the crook of the elbow is past the centerline of the opponent's neck. A shallow grip leaves the carotids untouched.

**Failing to break posture.** A guillotine on an opponent with tall posture finishes almost never. The head must be low and the spine must be curved forward.

**Opening the guard at the wrong time.** From closed guard, opening the legs and extending the hips is the finish — but doing it before the grip is locked lets the opponent escape by passing forward.

**Forgetting the opposite hand.** The non-choking hand plays a critical role: either gripping the choke hand to tighten the compression, or posting on the ground to control the finish angle.

## Defending the Guillotine

The guillotine is dangerous precisely because defenders often don't realize they are in it until the grip is locked. Clean defenses, in order of priority:

1. **Don't give up head position.** Any time you reach forward — for a hug, a clinch, a takedown shot — your head must stay above your hips. If your head dips, the guillotine is a step away.
2. **Posture up before the grip locks.** The moment you feel an arm wrapping your neck, drive your hips forward, straighten your spine, and lift your head. A half-locked guillotine dies against tall posture.
3. **Hand-fight the grip.** Two hands on the attacker's choking wrist, peeling down and away. Fighting the second hand's arrival (the one that would complete the grip) is the highest-priority defense.
4. **Get your free-side shoulder to the mat.** If you are in the opponent's closed guard with a guillotine locked, dropping your free-side shoulder to the mat and walking your head over to the choking arm side kills the choke angle.
5. **The "out the back door" escape.** From closed guard, if the guillotine is locked, the attacker will typically try to extend the hips. The defender counters by walking their knees forward on the choking arm side, effectively stacking the attacker and passing the guard while in the choke.
6. **Tap to a locked blood choke.** Riding out a Marcelotine is not a skill — it's a concussion waiting to happen.

## The Guillotine in MMA and Submission Grappling

The guillotine has finished more MMA fights than nearly any submission except the rear naked choke. The technique's ability to finish from the bottom, from standing, from the clinch, and from closed guard makes it the most positionally versatile submission in combat sports. Brian Ortega's guillotine victories in the UFC, Marcelo Garcia's Marcelotines in ADCC, Demian Maia's standing guillotine finishes, and Nate Diaz's wrecking-ball guillotine against Conor McGregor are all canonical examples of the technique at its highest level.

The common thread in every high-level guillotine finish is the attacker's ability to **feel the setup and commit instantly**. The guillotine is a fast-twitch attack — the window from "the head is low" to "the grip is locked" is often under half a second. Grapplers who hesitate miss the finish. Grapplers who commit without hesitation build careers around it.
