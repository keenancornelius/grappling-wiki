## Overview

The **heel hook** is a torsional attack on the knee joint where the attacker controls the opponent's heel and rotates the foot to twist the tibia against the femur, tearing the ligaments of the knee — primarily the **ACL**, **MCL**, **LCL**, **PCL**, and **meniscus**. It is the fastest-finishing, most dangerous, and most positionally versatile leg lock in grappling, and it has reshaped the modern submission grappling landscape more than any other technique of the past fifteen years.

Unlike most submissions, which finish joints that can signal pain — the elbow of an armbar, the shoulder of a kimura, the neck of a choke — the knee has **no pain response until it is already injured**. This is the defining property of the heel hook and the reason it is so dangerous. A practitioner caught in a heel hook often feels nothing, then feels a pop, then discovers they have torn multiple ligaments and will need surgery and six to twelve months of rehabilitation. There is no slow warning. The knee is fine until it isn't.

This is why heel hooks are illegal in traditional IBJJF gi competition and why they have been restricted at most belt levels until recently: they require partners to tap based on *positional understanding* rather than pain signals, and in live training they cause more serious injuries than any other submission in the sport when applied by inexperienced practitioners.

## The Two Heel Hooks: Inside vs Outside

There are two main heel hooks, and they attack the knee from opposite sides.

### The Outside Heel Hook

The **outside heel hook** rotates the foot laterally — away from the body's midline. It is the "classic" heel hook and has been legal in catch wrestling, sambo, and certain no-gi rulesets for decades. The attacker captures the heel with one arm and rotates the foot outward, twisting the tibia against the femur in the less-constrained direction of the knee's natural range. The outside heel hook is the slower of the two — the knee's outer ligaments resist the rotation for a fraction of a second longer — but it is still extraordinarily dangerous.

The outside heel hook is the heel hook of Rousimar Palhares, Rumina Sato, and the older sambo tradition. It finishes from [ashi garami](/wiki/ashi-garami), [50/50](/wiki/50-50-guard), and from what modern leg lockers call the "outside ashi" position.

### The Inside Heel Hook

The **inside heel hook** rotates the foot medially — toward the body's midline, with the toes pointing at the opponent's opposite hip. It is the **fastest-finishing submission in all of grappling**, capable of tearing multiple knee ligaments in under a second if the attacker commits fully to the rotation.

The inside heel hook attacks the knee through the **saddle position** (also called "inside sankaku" or "honey hole" or "411"). In this position, the attacker's legs entangle the opponent's trapped leg with a figure-four that isolates both the knee and the hip, leaving the opponent with no way to rotate out of the finish. This is the position that John Danaher and the Danaher Death Squad used to revolutionize no-gi submission grappling starting in the mid-2010s.

## The Leg Lock Revolution

Before 2015, leg locks were considered a secondary attack in high-level BJJ. The IBJJF banned most heel hooks, top-level competitors avoided the positions where they were available, and the conventional wisdom was that heel hooks were "cheap" — techniques that worked because they were surprising rather than because they were fundamentally sound.

This changed with **John Danaher**, the New Zealand-born coach working out of the Renzo Gracie academy in New York, and his students — **Eddie Cummings**, **Garry Tonon**, **Gordon Ryan**, **Craig Jones**, **Nicky Rodriguez**, and the rest of what came to be called the **Danaher Death Squad** (DDS). Danaher's innovation was not a new technique — it was a complete **system**: a set of entries, controls, and finishes built around the saddle position and the inside heel hook.

The DDS approach treated leg entanglements as primary positions, not desperation attacks. They drilled passes from saddle, transitions between leg positions, and escapes from their own legs being attacked. They proved that leg locks, when trained systematically, were the highest-percentage attacks in no-gi grappling — not the lowest.

The result was an absolute domination of ADCC and the no-gi competition scene starting in 2015. Eddie Cummings won ADCC 2015 with heel hook finishes. Garry Tonon built a famous highlight reel of sub-30-second leg lock finishes. Gordon Ryan became arguably the greatest grappler of his generation, largely on the strength of his leg lock system. Craig Jones took the same ideas and built a career as one of the most technical leg lockers in the sport.

Every major submission grappling academy in the world now teaches leg locks systematically, and the inside heel hook — once considered a dirty trick — is now the highest-percentage submission in professional no-gi competition. The revolution is complete.

## How the Heel Hook Finishes

The finish mechanics of the heel hook are deceptively simple: control the heel, control the knee, rotate. But each of those three elements has critical technical depth.

### The Four Requirements

1. **The heel must be captured, not the foot.** Controlling the midfoot or the ankle does not produce the torsional vector that tears the knee. The finishing grip is specifically on the heel — the hard bony protrusion at the bottom back of the foot.
2. **The opponent's knee must be isolated.** The attacker's legs must control the opponent's hip in a way that prevents them from rotating their whole body out of the lock. In the saddle position, this is accomplished by a figure-four of the attacker's legs. In ashi garami, it is accomplished by the bottom leg trapping the hip.
3. **The rotation comes from the attacker's body, not just the arms.** The finish is applied by the attacker turning their entire torso away from the opponent while holding the heel fixed. This uses bodyweight rather than arm strength, which is why a 140-pound leg locker can finish a 250-pound opponent.
4. **The opponent's foot cannot escape the pocket.** The attacker traps the opponent's foot in the pocket between their own armpit and ribcage, and the foot must stay there. Losing the foot means losing the finish.

## Setups and Entries

The heel hook is accessed through leg entanglements — positions where the attacker's legs interlock with the opponent's to create hip and knee control. The main leg entanglement families are:

### Ashi Garami

[Ashi garami](/wiki/ashi-garami) is the broad name for leg entanglements where one of the attacker's legs is between the opponent's legs and the other is outside. From ashi garami, the attacker can attack the outside heel hook on the entangled leg. This is the oldest legal heel hook position and the one most commonly used in rulesets that allow outside heel hooks only.

### The Saddle (Inside Sankaku, Honey Hole, 411)

The **[saddle](/wiki/saddle-position)** — also called "inside sankaku" in judo-derived grappling, "honey hole" by Eddie Bravo, and "411" by Danaher — is the position where the attacker's legs form a figure-four around the opponent's trapped leg. This position isolates the hip and knee simultaneously and produces the highest-percentage inside heel hook in grappling. The saddle is the home of the modern leg lock game and the position where most of the Danaher Death Squad finishes occurred.

### 50/50

The **[50/50 guard](/wiki/50-50-guard)** is a symmetric leg entanglement where both players have the same leg configuration on each other, and both players can attack the same heel hook simultaneously. 50/50 is notorious for its stalling potential in IBJJF gi competition but is one of the most dangerous leg-lock positions in no-gi, where it is often called a "race" — whoever finishes the heel hook first wins.

### Outside Ashi and Cross Ashi

Outside ashi puts the attacker's outside leg over the opponent's hip with the inside leg trapping the opponent's near leg. Cross ashi is a similar configuration from a different angle. Both positions enable outside heel hooks and are used when the saddle is not yet available.

## Common Mistakes

Most failed heel hooks come from the same errors:

**Controlling the foot instead of the heel.** The grip must be specifically on the heel, with the blade of the forearm running along the Achilles tendon. Grabbing the midfoot loses the torsional vector.

**Losing hip control.** If the opponent can rotate their hip out of the figure-four or ashi garami, the knee unwinds and the finish fails. Hip control is more important than the grip on the heel.

**Finishing with the arms.** Heel hooks are not finished by pulling on the heel — they are finished by rotating the torso with the heel locked in place. Arm-only finishes produce weak rotation and let the opponent escape.

**Failing to trap the foot.** The opponent's foot must be pinned against the attacker's body. If it escapes to the outside of the attacker's ribcage, the lock is gone.

**Giving the foot back during the setup.** Beginners often catch the heel, lose it during a transition, and never regain control. Heel hook chains demand constant grip maintenance.

## Defending the Heel Hook

Defending the heel hook is fundamentally about **never giving up the heel in the first place**. Once the heel is captured in a proper leg entanglement with good hip control, the finish is difficult to escape even for high-level practitioners.

Clean defenses, in order of priority:

1. **Don't give up the leg.** The entire leg lock game starts with someone giving up a leg — stepping into an ashi garami they didn't see, failing a pass and getting their leg caught, or getting rolled into a saddle. Training leg lock defense is largely training pass-and-retention mechanics.
2. **Hide the heel.** If your leg is already in a bad position, tuck your heel toward the attacker's body and your knee away from them. A hidden heel cannot be captured.
3. **Rotate with the attack.** If the attacker starts the rotation, rotating your own body with them preserves the knee by keeping the tibia and femur aligned. This is the "rolling" defense and is the last-second save that many high-level practitioners use when they realize they are about to be caught.
4. **Tap early.** Heel hooks are not techniques you can ride out. Once a competent attacker has the position and starts the rotation, tap before the pop. Heel hook injuries are surgical injuries.

## Heel Hook Training Culture and Safety

The Danaher Death Squad revolution came with a strong training culture around safe heel hook drilling. The rules most serious leg lock academies follow:

1. **No cranking.** Finishes are applied slowly, with position control first and rotation second. The goal in training is positional dominance, not a fast tap.
2. **Tap to position, not pain.** Because the heel hook has no pain signal, practitioners tap when they realize they are in a dominant control position with the heel captured, not when they feel discomfort.
3. **The attacker is responsible.** Experienced leg lockers stop applying pressure the moment they feel any resistance give way. Pushing through the pop is a sign of an incompetent or reckless training partner.
4. **Know your partners.** Heel hook training should happen between practitioners who know each other and trust each other's positional understanding.

Academies that follow these rules can train heel hooks with injury rates comparable to other submissions. Academies that don't produce the career-ending injuries that give leg locks their reputation.

## The Heel Hook in MMA

Heel hooks are legal in all major MMA promotions, and they finish fights with startling speed when they land. The most famous recent MMA heel hook finishes include **Ryan Hall's heel hook system** (which won him a UFC contract via The Ultimate Fighter), **Charles Rosa's leg lock finishes**, and **Rousimar Palhares' outside heel hook career**, which included multiple sub-30-second finishes at the UFC level.

The reason heel hooks do not dominate MMA as completely as they dominate submission grappling is that MMA allows strikes on the ground, and leg entanglements leave the attacker's head exposed to upkicks and the opponent's hands on the ground. Grapplers who can both defend strikes and attack leg locks are rare, which is why the few who can — Ryan Hall, Craig Jones in his ADCC appearances — have had outsized success.
