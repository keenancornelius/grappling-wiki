## Overview

The **Kimura** is a shoulder lock that rotates the opponent's arm behind their back against the natural range of the shoulder joint, threatening both the shoulder capsule and the elbow. It is named after **Masahiko Kimura**, the Japanese judo legend who used the technique to break Hélio Gracie's arm at the Maracanã Stadium in 1951 — a fight that defined the early history of Brazilian jiu-jitsu and gave the technique its Western name.

In judo, the technique is called **ude garami** ("entangled arm lock") and in catch wrestling it is the **double wristlock**. All three names describe the same mechanical configuration: a figure-four grip on the opponent's wrist, with the attacker's other arm threading behind the opponent's trapped arm and gripping their own wrist. The figure-four creates tremendous torque with almost no effort, and the lock can be applied from more positions than almost any other submission.

The Kimura is one of the four foundational shoulder attacks in grappling, alongside the [americana](/wiki/americana) (which rotates the arm in the opposite direction), the [omoplata](/wiki/omoplata) (a shoulder lock applied with the legs), and the [wristlock](/wiki/wrist-lock) (which attacks the joint below the elbow but chains from shoulder control). Together they form a complete attacking system for the upper body.

## How the Kimura Finishes

The Kimura is a **joint rotation** attack. Unlike the [armbar](/wiki/armbar), which hyperextends the elbow in a straight line, the Kimura rotates the humerus bone in the shoulder socket past its natural range. The shoulder joint is a ball-and-socket that has significant range of motion forward and to the sides but very limited rotation when the arm is bent behind the back — and that is exactly the vector the Kimura attacks.

Because the lock compounds through two joints (shoulder and elbow) with the figure-four grip acting as a lever system, the finishing force is multiplied far beyond what the attacker's muscles produce. A properly positioned Kimura can injure a much larger opponent with no strength advantage at all.

### The Four Requirements

A finishing Kimura needs four conditions to align:

1. **The figure-four grip is locked tight.** The attacker's choke-side hand grabs the opponent's wrist. The attacker's free arm threads over the opponent's trapped upper arm, between the upper arm and the body, and grips the attacker's own wrist. If the figure-four loosens at any point, the lever collapses.
2. **The opponent's elbow is bent at 90 degrees.** A straight arm turns the Kimura into a different attack (often a straight armbar or a kimura-to-armbar transition). For the classic finish, the elbow must stay bent.
3. **The attacker's chest is connected to the opponent's trapped shoulder.** Any space between the attacker's chest and the opponent's shoulder lets the shoulder rotate safely. Chest connection is the seal that makes the lock work.
4. **The opponent's hand is lifted toward their head, not the ground.** The finishing rotation takes the opponent's trapped hand upward and behind their own head, twisting the humerus in the socket. Pushing the hand toward the ground (or toward their feet) finishes nothing — it is the wrong rotational direction.

When these four are locked in, the finish happens with a small twist of the attacker's upper body. The Kimura is one of the few submissions where full-force finishing is genuinely dangerous — the shoulder can dislocate or the humerus can fracture before the opponent has time to tap if the attacker pulls too hard, too fast.

## The Kimura from Closed Guard

The closed guard Kimura is one of the first submission attacks every BJJ practitioner learns, and it remains effective at the highest levels of competition. The setup starts with the opponent posting a hand on the mat — a very common posture when they are in the bottom player's guard. The bottom player sits up on their elbow, grabs the posted wrist with the diagonal hand, threads their other arm over the opponent's upper arm and through to grip their own wrist, and locks the figure-four.

The critical detail in the closed guard Kimura is **angle creation**. Staying flat on your back while attempting the lock lets the opponent posture up and pop their arm free. Instead, the bottom player must turn their hips 45 to 90 degrees off center, load one shoulder toward the opponent, and pull the trapped arm across. This angle change is also the first move toward a sweep — if the Kimura doesn't finish, the same grip creates a high-percentage [hip bump sweep](/wiki/hip-bump-sweep) or a rollover to mount.

## The Kimura from Half Guard

The **half guard Kimura** is one of the most high-percentage submission attacks in all of modern no-gi grappling. From bottom half guard, when the top player uses a cross-face and drives their weight forward, the bottom player can hunt a Kimura grip on the far-side arm and use it to simultaneously:

- Submit the opponent if the lock finishes
- Sweep the opponent into mount if they defend the lock
- Take the back if they defend the sweep

This three-threat system is why half guard has become one of the most aggressive bottom positions in no-gi grappling. Eddie Bravo's "twister" system, Lucas Leite's half guard, and much of modern no-gi jiu-jitsu is built on hunting the Kimura grip from bottom half as a universal control.

The specific finishing mechanics from half guard involve getting to a sit-up position, isolating the far-side arm with the figure-four, and driving the opponent's trapped hand behind their own back. The sweep option comes from rolling through the Kimura grip rather than finishing — using the grip as a steering wheel to rotate the top player into bottom mount.

## The Kimura from Side Control and North-South

**Side control Kimura** is the most common top-position finish. From side control, when the opponent extends their far-side arm to frame on the attacker's hip or neck, the attacker slides their knee across the body, grabs the extended wrist, threads their other arm under the opponent's upper arm, and locks the figure-four. The finish requires the attacker to step over the opponent's head and rotate — the "North-South Kimura" variant — to close the rotation angle.

The **north-south Kimura** is one of Marcelo Garcia's signature techniques. By getting to north-south with the opponent's arm already controlled in the figure-four, the attacker has perfect rotational geometry — the opponent is fully flat and cannot roll out of the lock. Garcia's high-percentage finishing from this position is a hallmark of modern no-gi competition jiu-jitsu.

## The Kimura from Standing

Standing Kimura attacks — sometimes called "over-under" or "two-on-one" Kimura traps — are common in wrestling and judo. From an over-under clinch, the attacker can trap the underhook arm in a figure-four and either throw the opponent (judo's **ude garami nage**) or pull them into bottom guard with the Kimura grip already locked. This "Kimura trap" system, popularized by modern no-gi specialists, uses the grip itself as a takedown control rather than a submission in its first application.

The Kimura trap is one of the rare grip configurations in grappling that works for both offense and defense — it controls the opponent's upper body, threatens submission, and creates takedown and sweep options simultaneously.

## Common Mistakes

Most failed Kimuras come from these repeatable errors:

**Losing the figure-four during the finish.** If the figure-four grip breaks at any point, the lock collapses. Both hands must maintain grip throughout the entire sequence.

**Leaving a gap between chest and shoulder.** Chest connection is the seal that prevents the shoulder from rotating safely. Any daylight and the lock fails.

**Finishing in the wrong direction.** The trapped hand goes up and toward the opponent's head, not down and toward their feet. Rotating the wrong way does nothing.

**Letting the opponent straighten their arm.** A straight arm is a different attack. If the elbow straightens during the attempt, convert to a [straight armbar](/wiki/armbar) rather than fighting to re-bend the arm.

**Failing to control the opponent's hip.** From closed guard or half guard, if the opponent can step their leg free and posture up to their knees, they often escape even a deep Kimura. Hip control (usually with a leg pinned or a knee on the thigh) keeps the opponent from standing up.

**Ignoring the sweep option.** The Kimura grip is as valuable as a control as it is as a submission. Many closed guard or half guard Kimuras should convert into sweeps rather than forcing a low-percentage finish.

## Defending the Kimura

Kimura defenses fall into clear stages, from earliest to latest:

1. **Don't post your hand on the mat in closed guard.** This is the single most common way to give up a Kimura. Keep both hands off the ground when in someone's guard — grip their gi, post on their hip, or keep your hands near your own chest.
2. **Don't reach across your own centerline.** Reaching a far-side frame while being controlled gives the attacker a clean shot at the Kimura grip. Frame with the near-side arm instead.
3. **Break the figure-four early.** Once the figure-four grip is setting up, use your free hand to peel the attacker's grip before it locks. The "hand fight" on the figure-four is critical.
4. **Grab your own belt or opposite leg.** Once the lock is setting in, grabbing your own belt or thigh with the trapped hand creates a counter-grip that prevents rotation. This is the classic "hide the hand" defense.
5. **Turn into the attacker.** If the Kimura is setting up from side control, turning your chest into the attacker's chest (rather than away) eliminates the rotational angle and often kills the lock.
6. **Stand up.** From half guard, if you can get a post and stand all the way up before the finish lands, the Kimura is extremely difficult to finish against a standing opponent.

## The Kimura's History: Masahiko Kimura vs. Hélio Gracie

In 1951, the undisputed best judoka in the world, Masahiko Kimura, traveled to Brazil to fight Hélio Gracie in front of 20,000 spectators at the Maracanã Stadium. Kimura dominated the match, eventually trapping Gracie's arm in the figure-four shoulder lock that would later bear his name. When Gracie refused to tap, Kimura continued the rotation until Gracie's elbow broke — at which point Gracie's corner threw in the towel.

The fight cemented the technique's name in BJJ lore. Before Kimura beat Gracie, the lock was known simply as **ude garami** in judo. After the fight, Brazilian jiu-jitsu practitioners started calling it "the Kimura" in reference to the match, and the name has stuck for 75 years. Today, anywhere BJJ is practiced, "Kimura" means the figure-four shoulder lock — regardless of what language is being spoken.

Hélio Gracie reportedly said afterward that the loss was one of the most important moments of his career, because it gave him concrete proof of a technique his family had not fully developed. Within years, the Kimura was a staple of Gracie jiu-jitsu, and it has remained one of the most frequently trained submissions in every BJJ academy in the world.

## The Kimura in MMA

The Kimura has been the finishing technique of some of the most memorable MMA moments. **Frank Mir's Kimura of Antônio Rodrigo Nogueira** at UFC 140 — which broke Nogueira's arm on live television — is perhaps the single most famous Kimura finish in the sport's history. Kazushi Sakuraba, the "Gracie Hunter," finished multiple Gracie family members with Kimura-based attacks during his PRIDE career. **Tito Ortiz's Kimura of Ken Shamrock** and **Demian Maia's north-south Kimura system** are additional canonical references.

The Kimura's MMA success comes from the same property that makes it dangerous in sport jiu-jitsu: the figure-four grip works as both a control and a submission, which means the attacker can use it to stall, sweep, and submit without the risks of striking exchanges.
