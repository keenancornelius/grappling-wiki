## Overview

The **rear naked choke** — known as the **RNC** in English, **mata leão** ("lion killer") in Portuguese, and **hadaka jime** in Japanese — is the single most finished submission in combat sports history. It is a blood choke applied from behind the opponent that restricts blood flow through the carotid arteries on both sides of the neck. When correctly applied, it renders an opponent unconscious in an average of **8.9 seconds**, regardless of strength, pain tolerance, or training.

The word "naked" refers to the absence of the gi. Unlike a [cross collar choke](/wiki/cross-collar-choke) or a [bow and arrow choke](/wiki/bow-and-arrow-choke), the rear naked choke uses no clothing or grip on fabric — only the attacker's arms wrapping around the opponent's neck. This is why it is equally effective in Brazilian jiu-jitsu, no-gi submission grappling, judo, wrestling, MMA, and real-world self-defense.

In every major no-gi competition database and every statistical analysis of UFC finishes, the rear naked choke is the most frequent submission. It is the finish every grappler wants because it scales — from the beginner who just learned it to Marcelo Garcia hitting it in ADCC to Khabib Nurmagomedov closing out a title fight, the mechanics are identical.

## Where the Rear Naked Choke Lives in the Positional Hierarchy

The rear naked choke is the natural terminal endpoint of [back control](/wiki/back-control). It sits at the very bottom of grappling's causal chain: the attacker has already won every positional battle — they have passed guard, they have advanced to a dominant position, and they have taken the back. From back control with both hooks in and the seatbelt grip established, the rear naked choke is the highest-percentage attack available.

There is a reason the back is called the best position in jiu-jitsu. Unlike [mount](/wiki/mount) or [side control](/wiki/side-control), the opponent cannot see the attacks coming. Unlike [closed guard](/wiki/closed-guard), the defender is not facing the attacker and cannot frame, post, or posture up effectively. The face-to-back orientation removes the defender's ability to track threats, and the rear naked choke punishes this blindness.

Because it lives at the end of the chain, nearly every offensive system in grappling — passing, transitioning, scrambling, sweeping — eventually converges on the question: *can we get to the back and finish with the RNC?* The answer is yes far more often than any other submission-finish pairing in the sport.

## Mechanics: How the Rear Naked Choke Actually Finishes

The RNC is not a strength move. A properly applied rear naked choke finishes a 300-pound opponent as reliably as it finishes a 130-pound opponent, because it is not about compression force — it is about **vector alignment**. The choke works when two arteries get pinched simultaneously by the inside of the biceps and the inside of the forearm, while the back of the head and neck is pushed forward into the wedge the arms create.

### The Four Contact Points

There are four contact points that must all be correct for the choke to function:

1. **The crook of the elbow must be centered under the chin.** If the elbow is off to one side of the neck, the choking arm compresses the trachea instead of the carotid arteries. This turns a blood choke into an air choke, which is much slower, much more painful, and much easier to defend.
2. **The biceps of the choking arm must press on one carotid.** This is the inside of the front of the upper arm, not the back of it.
3. **The inside of the forearm (radial side) must press on the other carotid.** The bony edge of the forearm makes the cleanest contact.
4. **The free hand pushes the head forward into the wedge.** Without this forward pressure, the opponent can pull their head back and relieve the choke. The head must be driven into the V created by the choking arm's biceps and forearm.

When all four points are aligned, the choke requires almost no force. Grapplers sometimes describe a correct RNC as "closing the angle" rather than "squeezing." You are not strangling someone — you are folding a geometric wedge shut around their neck.

### The Two Grips

There are two standard grip configurations for finishing the rear naked choke:

**Palm-to-biceps grip (standard).** The choking arm wraps around the neck. The choking hand grabs the biceps of the non-choking arm. The non-choking hand goes flat on the back of the opponent's head, pushing the head forward. This is the version taught in most BJJ academies. It is versatile and works against most defensive positions.

**Palm-to-palm / hands clasped (figure-four).** The hands clasp each other rather than gripping the biceps. This version concentrates pressure more narrowly and is often used when the opponent is actively defending by grabbing the choking arm. It is harder for the defender to peel because there is no exposed biceps to grip. John Danaher's systems lean heavily on variations of this grip.

Both grips finish the same way. The choice is situational and personal.

### The Squeeze

A correctly positioned rear naked choke is finished by squeezing the elbows together, flaring the chest, and pushing the head forward — all at the same time. Think of it as trying to touch your elbows in front of your chest while pulling your choking hand toward the opposite shoulder. The back arches slightly, the chest expands, and the choke tightens as a single coordinated contraction. Pulling the arm straight back without these other elements is a weak choke.

## The Path to the Rear Naked Choke

The rear naked choke only happens if you get to back control with both hooks and a seatbelt. Getting there is the hard part. Once you are there, finishing is relatively straightforward — which is why most of the technical work around the RNC is really about how to take the back, not how to finish once you have it.

### From Turtle

The most common entry to back control in both BJJ and wrestling is from the [turtle position](/wiki/turtle-position). When an opponent shells up after a failed sweep, a bad scramble, or a pass attempt, they are one body lock and two hooks away from giving up the back.

### From Mount

Every time the opponent turns to their side under mount to escape, the back is available. Mounted opponents often turn because they panic under pressure — that turn is an invitation to take the back. This is the classic "bump and take the back" sequence.

### From Side Control

The opponent's top-side arm can be trapped to facilitate back takes. A common path is to pin the far arm across the body while creating an angle and sliding behind.

### From the Guard (Top and Bottom)

The [berimbolo](/wiki/berimbolo) is the most famous modern guard-to-back transition, used extensively in IBJJF competition. The arm drag from [butterfly guard](/wiki/butterfly-guard) is a wrestling-derived entry. The [rolling back take from De La Riva](/wiki/de-la-riva-guard) is another common path.

### From Standing

Rear body lock takedowns, snap downs to back exposure, and bear hug reversals all put the attacker behind the opponent on the feet. Getting the back standing is often the fastest route to the RNC when the opponent is taller or stronger.

## Common Mistakes

Most failed rear naked choke attempts fail for the same small set of reasons. All of them are fixable.

**Choking on the throat, not the arteries.** If the opponent says it hurts rather than feeling lightheaded, you are on the throat. Adjust until the crook of the elbow is centered under the chin.

**Leaving the choking elbow flared out.** A flared elbow gives the defender something to grab. Keep the choking elbow pointed down or tucked toward your own body.

**Not hiding the choking hand.** Exposing the choking hand lets the defender peel it off. The choking hand should disappear into the biceps grip or be clasped palm-to-palm with the other hand immediately.

**Losing the seatbelt grip when transitioning to the choke.** The seatbelt grip (one arm over the shoulder, one under the armpit) controls the opponent and keeps the chest sealed to their back. Abandoning the seatbelt prematurely gives the opponent space to turn into you and escape the back.

**Losing hooks while finishing.** If the hooks come out during the choke attempt, the opponent can escape the back and turn into guard. Maintain both hooks or switch to a body triangle to lock in body control before committing to the choke.

**Squeezing the opponent's jaw instead of their neck.** If your forearm is too high, you are squeezing their chin and teeth. The forearm must be low on the neck, under the chin, against the soft tissue where the carotids run.

**Finishing from too high on the back.** The attacker's chest must stay sealed to the opponent's upper back. If you ride too high — up on their head and shoulders — the leverage is gone and the opponent can shrug you off.

## Defending and Escaping the Rear Naked Choke

Escaping a fully locked-in rear naked choke from a skilled attacker is extraordinarily difficult. The realistic goal is to prevent the choke from ever locking in. Once the second hand joins the choke, you have perhaps three to five seconds before you go unconscious.

### Prevention (Before the Choke Is In)

**Protect your neck.** The moment you feel the attacker's arm sliding under your chin, drop your chin to your chest and bring both hands to your neck. Your chin acts as a wedge blocking the choking arm from sliding across the throat. Your hands peel or fight the choking arm.

**Fight the second hand.** The rear naked choke requires two hands — the choking arm and the finishing hand. Your job is to keep the second hand from joining. Two-on-one grip on the choking arm, fighting wrist control, and shoulder shrugging all delay the second hand.

**Escape the back first.** If you can get your shoulders to the mat (a "hip-to-hip" escape, rolling out to one side and pinning the attacker's knee) before the choke sinks, you reach a neutral or defensive position instead of losing consciousness.

### Late Defenses (Choke Is Sinking)

**Tuck your chin and hand-fight.** This delays the finish but rarely prevents it entirely against a strong choke.

**Tap early.** Going unconscious from a rear naked choke is not a badge of honor. It carries real neurological risk. Once you feel your vision tunnel or hear the ringing sound that precedes a blackout, tap — you have already lost the position, and the tap preserves your training longevity.

## The Rear Naked Choke in MMA

No submission has finished more UFC fights than the rear naked choke. The reason is simple: MMA fighters often lose the back without training to defend it, and strikes on the ground punish any attempt to hand-fight with one's arms away from the face. In a grappling match, you can fully commit to preventing the second hand from joining the choke. In MMA, committing both hands to neck defense leaves your head exposed to punches, so defenders routinely give up the neck to protect their face — and the RNC lands.

Khabib Nurmagomedov's UFC career is the clearest demonstration of this dynamic. His grappling system was built around taking the back and finishing with the rear naked choke, and the fighters he faced were forced to choose between defending the choke and defending punches. The choke won almost every time.

## The Rear Naked Choke in Self-Defense

The RNC is one of the most practically useful techniques in all of grappling for self-defense precisely because it does not depend on strikes, does not require the user to be larger or stronger, and finishes in seconds. A 140-pound practitioner can render a 250-pound attacker unconscious with a properly applied rear naked choke. This is not true of most striking techniques.

For the same reason, the RNC carries significant responsibility. A rear naked choke held past unconsciousness can cause serious injury or death. Any grappler teaching the technique to students for self-defense should emphasize that the goal is to neutralize, not to harm, and that releasing the choke after the opponent loses consciousness is essential.

## Variations and Related Chokes

The rear naked choke is the parent of several related techniques:

- **[Bow and Arrow Choke](/wiki/bow-and-arrow-choke)** — a gi-based back choke that uses the collar instead of the naked arm. Often used when the attacker has the back but cannot get under the chin for the RNC.
- **[Arm Triangle from the Back](/wiki/arm-triangle)** — when the defender traps the choking arm, an arm triangle sometimes becomes available.
- **[North-South Choke](/wiki/north-south-choke)** — a different blood choke, but uses the same biceps-on-carotid principle applied from the top.

## Training the Rear Naked Choke

Progress on the rear naked choke comes from drilling three things, in this order:

1. **Back control maintenance.** Spend time in back control with a resisting partner and focus on keeping the seatbelt, keeping both hooks, and keeping your chest sealed to their back. If you cannot hold the back, the choke is irrelevant.
2. **Neck threading.** Practice getting the choking arm under the chin against defensive hand-fighting. This is the "hard part" of the finish — most finishes are one-arm-on, one-arm-fighting situations.
3. **Finishing mechanics.** Drill the squeeze on a partner with soft taps. Learn what a correctly aligned RNC feels like — it finishes with almost no effort — versus a misaligned one that requires straining.

Rear naked choke training is safe when partners tap early. It is dangerous when ego overrides awareness. Tap early, drill often, and the choke will become the highest-percentage finish in your game.
