## Overview

The **triangle choke** — **sankaku jime** in judo — is a blood choke applied with the legs rather than the arms. The attacker traps one of the opponent's arms and the head inside a triangular configuration formed by their own thighs. The crook of one knee locks behind the opposite ankle, and the angle of the trapped arm forces the opponent's own shoulder to compress one carotid artery while the attacker's thigh compresses the other. Unconsciousness follows in roughly the same timeframe as a [rear naked choke](/wiki/rear-naked-choke) — under ten seconds if the choke is correctly locked.

The triangle is one of the three brothers of guard attacks, alongside the [armbar](/wiki/armbar) and [omoplata](/wiki/omoplata). These three submissions share geometry — any one of them can chain into either of the others — and learning the triangle deeply is essentially learning half of closed guard offense.

The triangle is unique among major submissions in that it uses the attacker's **own legs** to finish, freeing the hands to control posture, grip the head, or attack simultaneously with an [armbar](/wiki/armbar) or a wrist lock on the trapped arm. This dual-threat nature is why the triangle has become the single most iconic submission in Brazilian jiu-jitsu: Rickson Gracie, Roger Gracie, Marcelo Garcia, Ryan Hall, and Nicky Ryan are all famous for their triangle systems, and each of them treats it as the terminal end of a broader attacking ecosystem rather than a single move.

## How the Triangle Choke Finishes

The triangle is not about squeezing the legs. It is about **geometry**. A correctly locked triangle finishes on a world-class heavyweight with nothing but positioning — no muscular exertion. A poorly locked triangle, even with tremendous leg strength, fails against a purple belt.

### The Four Requirements

A finishing triangle requires four things to all be true:

1. **One arm in, one arm out.** This is the single most common mistake — both arms must be separated. The arm that is "in" has the shoulder and upper arm trapped inside the triangle. The arm that is "out" is completely outside the triangle. If both arms are inside, it's a body lock with your legs, not a choke. If neither arm is inside, you can't choke the neck because the angle is wrong.
2. **The knee is across the back of the neck, not across the head.** If the choking leg is riding high on the opponent's skull, there's no compression on the carotid. The leg must be low, pressing on the back of the neck at the base of the skull.
3. **The ankle is locked behind the opposite knee.** The finishing position is your knee crossing behind your other leg's knee — not ankle-to-ankle. An ankle-to-ankle figure-four is weaker and easier to escape.
4. **The opponent's trapped arm is pulled across their own centerline.** This is the detail that separates finishing triangles from held triangles. Pulling the arm across makes the opponent's own shoulder press their own carotid. Without the cross-pull, you are only choking with one side.

When all four are aligned, the finish requires no pulling or squeezing. You just angle your hips slightly, lock the knee across, and the opponent taps.

## The Triangle from Closed Guard

The classic triangle setup comes from [closed guard](/wiki/closed-guard). The attacker breaks the opponent's posture down, gets a grip on one wrist, and pulls that arm across while pushing the opposite arm up with a knee. This creates the "one in, one out" configuration. The attacker then swings the far leg up and over the trapped shoulder, creating the first leg of the triangle, and closes the lock by pulling the shin behind their own knee.

The critical moment in this setup is the **angle creation**. A triangle attempted square to the opponent rarely finishes. The attacker must turn their hips 45 to 90 degrees off center, so the choke leg is coming down across the opponent's neck at an angle rather than flat. This angle change is what Roger Gracie built his entire closed guard game around, and why his triangle had a higher finishing percentage than nearly anyone else in the sport during his competitive peak.

## The Mounted Triangle

From [mount](/wiki/mount), the triangle is a terrifying attack because the opponent cannot use gravity to stack you — you are already on top. The **mounted triangle** starts from high mount, with the attacker isolating one of the opponent's arms and swinging the far leg around behind their head. The finish is often devastating because the opponent has no posture to use and no hip mobility to escape.

The mounted triangle chains constantly with the mounted armbar and the [arm triangle](/wiki/arm-triangle). If the opponent defends the triangle by keeping both arms in, the attacker lands an arm triangle. If they defend by extending the trapped arm, the attacker falls back for a mounted juji gatame. The three attacks share a single setup, which is why the mounted triangle is one of the highest-percentage submission sequences in grappling.

## The Reverse Triangle

The **reverse triangle** (sometimes called the "inverted triangle") is a triangle applied from the opposite direction — typically with the attacker facing the opponent's legs instead of their head. It is set up from north-south, from side control, or from the turtle position. Mechanics-wise, it is still a leg triangle trapping head plus one arm, but the angle of the choke comes from the opposite side. The reverse triangle is a favorite of leg-lock specialists because it integrates with [leg entanglements](/wiki/ashi-garami) — you can threaten a reverse triangle and a heel hook at the same time.

## The Side Triangle

A lesser-known but high-percentage variation is the **side triangle** — a triangle locked from side control or knee on belly rather than closed guard. The attacker isolates the far arm, swings one leg over the opponent's head while the other leg controls the hip, and finishes perpendicular to the opponent's spine. This version is common in leglock chains and is essentially the "head-and-arm" version of the triangle family.

## Common Mistakes

Most failed triangles come from a small set of repeatable errors:

**Both arms in (or both out).** This is the most common error by a wide margin. The choke cannot finish if the configuration is symmetric. One arm must be cleanly trapped inside, the other cleanly out.

**Square hips.** Finishing square to the opponent means fighting their entire bodyweight. The angle must come first, even before you close the lock.

**Choking with the shin instead of the thigh.** If the choke pressure is from the shin bone, you are trying to saw the opponent's neck, which is slow and painful but rarely finishes. The pressure must come from the thigh — the meaty part of the quadriceps against the carotid.

**Failing to pull the arm across.** Without the arm cross-pull, half the choke is missing. The opponent can survive indefinitely with nothing but neck flexion.

**Letting the opponent stand up.** If the opponent can posture all the way to their feet with the triangle locked, they can slam or simply unfold the lock by straightening their back. Keeping the opponent's head down throughout the lock-up is critical.

**Locking ankle-to-ankle instead of knee-over-ankle.** A figure-four at the ankles is weaker and can be pried open. The correct lock is the shin crossing behind the opposite knee.

## Defending the Triangle Choke

The triangle has clear defensive stages, and the earliest defenses are by far the most reliable:

1. **Prevent the setup.** Keep your arms and head from entering the one-in-one-out configuration. Either both hands inside on the hips (stacking posture) or both hands outside on the mat — never one of each.
2. **Posture up immediately.** The moment you feel a leg coming over your shoulder, stand up and straighten your spine. Triangles cannot finish against a standing opponent with good posture, because the geometry is impossible to close.
3. **Stack and walk around.** Drive forward with your shoulders, press the attacker's knees into their chest, and try to walk your feet around to the side where the triangle leg is. This brings the attacker's back onto the mat and kills the angle.
4. **Hide the trapped arm.** Grab your own thigh, collar, or opposite biceps with the trapped arm. A bent, hidden arm cannot be used to finish the choke because the "arm across" detail can't be set.
5. **The leg-over-shoulder escape.** If the lock is fully closed and the arm is across, sliding the trapped arm down the attacker's thigh while walking toward the choking leg is a late-stage escape that buys seconds.
6. **Tap before the fade.** Like all blood chokes, a held triangle goes from uncomfortable to unconscious in seconds. Do not ride out a locked triangle — tap and reset.

## The Triangle in MMA

In MMA, the triangle has been decisive in some of the sport's most iconic finishes. **Anderson Silva's triangle choke of Chael Sonnen** at UFC 117 — coming from the bottom of a mount he had been crushed in for four rounds — is one of the most famous submissions in combat sports history because of its context and timing. **Renzo Gracie's triangle-armbar on Ben Spaulding**, **Fabricio Werdum's triangle of Fedor Emelianenko**, and **Demian Maia's triangle choke of Chael Sonnen** are canonical demonstrations of the closed guard triangle setup working at the highest level of competition.

The lesson from MMA triangles is that the technique works even when the attacker is losing the fight, because it only requires the opponent to briefly lean in with one arm while defending strikes. The triangle's ability to finish from the worst position in grappling — the bottom of mount or the bottom of side control — is what makes it different from nearly every other submission in the sport.

## Triangle as a System

Roger Gracie, Nicky Ryan, and Ryan Hall each built entire competition games around the triangle rather than treating it as a single technique. In these systems, the triangle is the backbone of a broader attacking framework:

- **The [armbar](/wiki/armbar) chains off every missed triangle.** If the opponent defends by extending the arm that was supposed to stay inside, it becomes a juji gatame.
- **The [omoplata](/wiki/omoplata) chains off every triangle that gets stacked.** When the opponent posture-escapes, the attacker rotates their hips and lands the shoulder lock.
- **The sweep chains off every triangle that gets escaped.** The omoplata or arm drag off a failed triangle often ends with a sweep into a dominant top position, trading a missed submission for a positional advance.

Building a triangle system means training these chains until they are reflexive — not hunting a single triangle attempt, but flowing through the whole family of attacks until something finishes.
